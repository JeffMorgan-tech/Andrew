SCENE 1 Cold Open — Tuesday Morning, Ticket Review
ANDREW is on a mentoring call with LUKE and ANTHONY. He's reviewing their recent tickets — a weekly ritual that has become steadily less painful over the last few weeks. The new hires are improving. Slowly. But visibly. ANDREW is feeling confident. Maybe too confident. The kind of confident that comes right before the universe reminds you who's in charge.
Andrew
Alright guys, looking at this week's tickets — not bad. Luke, your P3 from yesterday? Solid documentation. Timestamps, steps, customer confirmation. That's clean work.
Luke
(genuinely proud) Thanks, man. I've been using that template Corine made.
Andrew
Anthony, your VPN ticket — the resolution notes are clear. Good stuff. One thing though — on ticket 4471, the customer's Eclipse integration is throwing sync errors after the patch. I see you noted that you were going to try rolling back the config and re-syncing manually. But — I think that's the wrong approach. That's going to take way too long. The faster fix is to push a fresh config from the admin side and force a re-auth. I've done it a hundred times. Trust me on this one.
Anthony
(hesitant) Oh — okay. I mean, I talked to Juwan about it yesterday and he said the rollback is actually safer because the fresh push can sometimes break the custom auth module if the site has legacy integrations. He said he saw it happen on an ARIA site last year.
Andrew
(waving it off, confident) Nah, that's an edge case. Ninety-nine percent of the time the fresh push works fine. Juwan's being overly cautious. Let's go with the push. I'll walk you through it.
ANTHONY nods. He doesn't look convinced, but he trusts Andrew. LUKE watches quietly. Something about this feels off to both of them, but neither says anything. They're new. Andrew's the mentor. Andrew knows best.

SCENE 2 Act One — "The Push"
An hour later. ANDREW has walked ANTHONY through the fresh config push. ANTHONY executes it. For a moment, everything looks fine. Green status across the board. ANDREW leans back, satisfied.
Andrew
See? Clean. Fast. That's how it's done. Juwan's rollback would've taken forty-five minutes. This took four.
Anthony
(watching the screen) Yeah, it looks — wait. What's that?
A red banner appears at the top of the customer's system dashboard. "AUTH MODULE ERROR — LEGACY INTEGRATION CONFLICT." The exact thing Juwan warned about.
♪ A SINGLE, DEVASTATING ERROR CHIME ♪
Andrew
(staring at the screen) ...Oh no.
Luke
(reading the error) "Legacy integration conflict." Isn't that what Juwan said would happen?
Andrew
(very quiet) ...Yes. That is what Juwan said would happen.
Laugh track
Anthony
So... we broke it?
Andrew
(pinching the bridge of his nose) I broke it. I told you to do the push. This one's on me.
The silence that follows is the silence of a mentor realizing, in front of the people he's supposed to be teaching, that he was wrong. And not a little wrong. Juwan-tried-to-tell-him wrong.
Laugh track
SCENE 3 "The Cleanup"
ANDREW immediately calls JUWAN. It takes one ring.
Juwan
(picking up, calm, no judgment) What happened?
Andrew
I pushed the fresh config on the Eclipse site. The one Anthony asked you about. The one you said to roll back instead. It broke the auth module. You were right.
Juwan
(beat) ...The legacy integration?
Andrew
The legacy integration.
Juwan
(not gloating, just steady) Alright. Pull up the admin console. I'll walk you through the recovery. We'll do the rollback first, then re-sync the auth module manually. It'll take about forty-five minutes.
Andrew
(exhaling) Forty-five minutes. Exactly what it would've taken if I'd listened in the first place.
Juwan
(small grin) Funny how that works.
Laugh track
They fix it. It takes forty-seven minutes. ANTHONY and LUKE shadow the whole thing — silently, attentively. They watch Andrew ask for help. They watch Juwan give it without rubbing it in. They watch two peers navigate a mistake like professionals. It's the best lesson they've had all month, and nobody planned it.
SCENE 4 Act Two — "The Spiral"
Afternoon. The site is fixed. The customer is happy. But ANDREW isn't. He's at his desk, headset off, staring at the ticket notes he wrote: "Root cause: incorrect remediation approach selected by support (me). Corrected via rollback per Juwan's original recommendation." He wrote it honestly. And it's eating him alive.
Andrew
(DM to Devon) Hey. Quick question. Have you ever been completely wrong about something in front of the people you were supposed to be training?
Devon
(DM back, immediately) Every week for eight years. Welcome to the club.
Laugh track
Andrew
(DM) How do you deal with it?
Devon
(DM) I stopped pretending I wasn't wrong. That's it. That's the whole thing. The moment you try to protect your image as the guy who knows everything, you become the guy who can't be trusted. The new hires don't need you to be right all the time, Andrew. They need you to be honest all the time. Those are different jobs.
The most words Devon has typed in a single message all season. ANDREW reads it twice.
Andrew
(DM) ...That's actually really good advice, Devon.
Devon
(DM) Don't make it a thing.
Laugh track
Meanwhile, in the team channel:
Jeff M.
(DM to Andrew, unprompted) I heard about the config push. For what it's worth, I made the same mistake in my first year. Except I did it on a production Eclipse environment and it took down treatment planning for six hours. I didn't tell anyone for two of those hours because I thought I could fix it myself. I could not fix it myself. The point is: you're fine. Also, I sent you a meme about it. Check your phone at 2 AM.
Big laugh
SCENE 5 Act Three — "Rachelle's Office"
End of day. RACHELLE pings ANDREW for a one-on-one. Her camera is on. Soft lighting. The bookshelf. A faint clucking sound in the background — she's near the chicken run. She's read the ticket notes. She knows what happened.
Rachelle
So. I saw the ticket.
Andrew
(bracing) Yeah. I messed up. Anthony had the right approach — well, Juwan's approach — and I overrode it. The config push broke the auth module. We fixed it, but it shouldn't have happened. I should've listened.
Rachelle
(nodding slowly) You should have. And you know that now. But Andrew — I want to tell you something, and I want you to really hear it. What happened today? That's not a failure. That's one of the most valuable things you could've given Luke and Anthony.
Andrew
(confused) ...Breaking a customer's system is valuable?
Rachelle
(smiling gently) Showing them that their mentor can be wrong — and admit it — is invaluable. Andrew, think about what they saw today. They saw you make a call. They saw it go bad. They saw you own it immediately — not deflect, not blame the system, not pretend it didn't happen. You called Juwan. You asked for help. You wrote honest ticket notes. You let them watch the whole thing. Do you know how rare that is?
Andrew
(quiet) It didn't feel valuable. It felt embarrassing.
Rachelle
Of course it did. That's what growth feels like. It feels embarrassing and uncomfortable and like you want to crawl under your desk. But here's the truth: if Luke and Anthony only ever see you being right, they're going to be terrified of being wrong. And in this job — in any job — being wrong is not the problem. Staying wrong is. You didn't stay wrong today. You fixed it. You owned it. That's the lesson.
ANDREW is quiet for a moment. Really processing it.
Rachelle
Don't take it too hard. You're a good mentor, Andrew. Not because you're always right — but because when you're wrong, you don't hide it. That's the kind of person I'd want teaching me.
Audience: "Aww"
Andrew
(small, genuine) ...Thanks, Rachelle.
Rachelle
(warm) You know who else had to learn this lesson? Dolly.
Andrew
...The chicken?
Rachelle
She kept trying to roost on the water dish instead of the perch. Every night. Fell in every time. But last week? She figured out the perch. And she figured it out because I let her fall. Sometimes the fall is the lesson.
Andrew
(beat) Rachelle, did you just compare me to a baby chicken?
Rachelle
(perfect smile) I compared you to a baby chicken who learned.
Big laugh
