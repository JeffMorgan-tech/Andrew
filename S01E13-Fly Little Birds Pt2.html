We pick up exactly where we left off. The P1 bridge call. ANTHONY is on the line. The customer's ARIA system is down. GREG from SDS has just been asked to check for backend changes. Andrew's webcam box still reads "Disconnected." The silence is deafening.
GREG comes back on the line.
Greg
(grudgingly, like every word costs him money) ...Alright. So. It looks like there was a schema update pushed last night from our side. It was supposed to go to staging but it went to production. That could be causing the connection timeout.

A webcam box on the call that has been dark for the entire season — thirteen episodes, every standup, every meeting, every crisis — suddenly flickers to life. DEVON's camera turns on.
The team chat explodes silently. Everyone watching sees it at the same time. Devon. On camera. For the first time. He's in his fifties. Salt-and-pepper stubble. A dim room in Vermont. One desk lamp. The same chair from 2007. He looks exactly like you'd imagine — tired, steady, and completely done.
He looks directly into the camera. He's been listening the whole time.
Devon
(on camera, voice flat, absolute zero temperature) Greg. I'm going to stop you right here.

Devon
I've been on this team for eight years. I've worked more P1s than you've filed. I've written knowledge articles that your own engineers reference when they're stuck. I have been listening to you talk down to people on this team for longer than some of these new hires have been out of college. And I'm done listening.
GREG opens his mouth. Nothing comes out.
Devon
Anthony asked you a clear, specific, technically sound question. And instead of answering it, you tried to blame his team for a schema update that your side pushed to production by accident. He caught it. Not you. Him. The new guy. On his first solo day. While his mentor's internet was down. And you're sitting there calling him "your guy" and questioning whether he can handle the call.
Complete silence on the bridge.
Devon
(leaning forward, looking directly into the camera — the first time anyone on this team has seen his face, and it is delivering the performance of the season) He can handle the call. He just did. Standing ovation

Anthony
(privately, DM to Devon) Devon. Thank you. I don't even know what to say.
Devon
(DM back) You don't need to say anything. You did the work. I just made sure nobody got in your way. That's what the team is for.

Big laugh
SCENE 2 Act One — "Meanwhile, in Luke's World"
Simultaneously. LUKE has been working through his P2 Eclipse ticket. Jeff's wall-of-text DM got him to the version mismatch. He walked the customer through the update. But now there's a second issue — the sync still isn't completing even after the version update. He's stuck. And he's panicking.
Luke
(DM to Jeff M.) Jeff — the version update went through but the sync is still failing. The customer's getting impatient. I don't know what to try next. The KB article doesn't cover this scenario. What do I do??
Jeff M.
(DM back) Okay so there are fourteen possible causes for a post-update sync failure in Eclipse 16.x. I'll list them in order of probability. Number one: residual cache from the previous version. Flush the Eclipse application cache — there's a utility in the admin tools panel under Advanced > Cache Management. Number two: the sync service might need a restart after the version change. Number three: check if the treatment planning module has a pending license validation — sometimes the license handshake stalls after a major version jump. Number four: examine the database replication logs for — actually hold on. I'm going to send you my full troubleshooting decision tree. It's forty-three nodes. I built it last month. Number five —
LUKE stares at the messages pouring in. There are now eight consecutive DMs from Jeff. Each one is a paragraph. LUKE's eyes are glazing over. He can feel the customer waiting. He needs to move fast, not read a novel.
Luke
(DM to Jeff) Jeff — thank you. Seriously. This is incredible. I need to go simpler though. Is there someone who can walk me through it live? Like, on a call?
Jeff M.
(DM back, instantly) Carlos. Call Carlos. He's dealt with more Eclipse sync issues than anyone on the team. He'll know the fastest path. Tell him I said the cache flush is step one. He'll agree. Also — I'm proud of you for asking. That's not weakness. That's efficiency. Now go. And check the meme later.
Laugh track
LUKE calls CARLOS. One ring.
Carlos
(picking up, calm as always, coffee probably in hand) Hey Luke. What's going on?
Luke
(talking fast, a little frantic) Carlos — I'm on a P2, Eclipse sync failure, customer's waiting, I did the version update per KB-4471, sync still won't complete, Jeff sent me like forty-seven possible causes but I don't know where to start, Andrew's offline, and I think the customer is getting frustrated because I've had them on hold twice and —
Carlos
(cutting in, gentle, unhurried) Luke. Slow down. Take a breath.
LUKE stops. Breathes.
Carlos
Good. Now — Jeff's right about the cache. That's your first move. Flush the application cache, restart the sync service, and give it two minutes. Nine times out of ten, that's all it needs after a version change. If it doesn't work, we'll try the license handshake. But let's start simple. One step at a time. You've got this, young blood.
Luke
(calmer already) ...Okay. Cache flush. Restart sync. Two minutes.
Carlos
That's it. I'll stay on the line. Go ahead.
LUKE goes back to the customer. He walks them through the cache flush. He restarts the sync service. He waits. One minute. Ninety seconds. Two minutes. And then — the sync completes. Green across the board. The data flows. The ticket is resolved.
Luke
(exhaling, voice cracking slightly) Carlos... it worked.
Carlos
(smiling, you can hear it) Of course it did. You did the work, Luke. I just told you which button to press first. That's all mentoring is — someone who's already pressed the wrong button a hundred times telling you which one to press next. You did great today.
Applause
Luke
(genuine) Thanks, Carlos. And tell Jeff thanks too. Even if his DMs gave me a panic attack.

(laughing) That's just Jeff, man. His heart's in the right place. It's just... a lot of place.

SCENE 5 Act Three — "Andrew Comes Home"

He's called the ISP twice from a neighbor's phone. He's paced his entire house. He considered driving to a coffee shop but didn't want to miss the moment his connection came back.
And then — at 4:47 PM — the modem lights blink. One. Two. Three. Green. The Wi-Fi icon on his laptop flickers. Reconnects. Teams loads. His inbox floods.
Andrew
(scrambling to his desk, opening everything at once) Come on, come on, come on —
He opens the ticket dashboard. The P1 bridge call ticket: RESOLVED. Resolved by Anthony. Notes: clean, detailed, timestamped. Root cause: accidental schema push from SDS. Resolution: rollback by engineering, confirmed by support. Customer status: satisfied.
He opens Luke's tickets. The P2: RESOLVED. Resolved by Luke. Notes: version mismatch identified via KB-4471, updated, cache flushed, sync confirmed. Documented. Flagged for follow-up. Clean.
Both tickets closed. Both resolved correctly. Both fully documented. Both done without him.

Post-Credit Scene 1
Late night. JEFF M.'s home office. The house is quiet — the three-year-old is asleep, the teenagers have retreated to their rooms, and his wife has gone to bed. The treadmill is off. Jeff is sitting. Perfectly still. Three monitors glowing. On the center screen: lines of code. On the left screen: a knowledge base article. On the right screen: a chat interface.
He types a command. Hits enter. Waits. The chat interface flickers. A response appears — generated not by a person, but by something Jeff has been building for months. Something that just ingested every knowledge article in the company's SharePoint library. Something that can search, summarize, and retrieve technical answers in seconds.
It works. It actually works.
Jeff M.
(whispering, staring at the screen, the glow reflecting off his face) ...Hello, BookBot.
The cursor blinks. BookBot responds: "Hello, Jeff. How can I help you today?"
Jeff leans back. For the first time all season, the treadmill man is completely still. He smiles. Then — because he's Jeff — he types one more thing.
Jeff M.
(typing into BookBot) Can you send memes?
BookBot responds: "I can be configured to deliver scheduled content to specified recipients at designated intervals."
Jeff M.
(the biggest smile of the season) ...We're going to get along just fine.
