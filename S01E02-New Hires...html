ANTHONY's times are more consistent, hovering around 9:08 to 9:15, which would be fine except for the fact that his Teams status doesn't go green until roughly thirty minutes after he clocks in, which suggests a Starbucks detour is involved.

Andrew
(opening eyes, decision made) I need backup.

SCENE 2 Act One — "The Mentor Meeting"
A private Teams call. No new hires — just the veterans. ANDREW, TINA, DEVON, and JEFF M. This is the mentor meeting: an informal, off-the-books call where people who've been around the block compare notes on training the next generation. TINA has a fresh cup of tea. DEVON's camera is off. JEFF M. is walking on his treadmill and has already shared his screen to show a graph that no one asked for.
Andrew
Alright, I called this because I'm three weeks in with Luke and Anthony and I'm starting to lose it. They're not clocking in on time. Their ticket notes are basically haikus — bad haikus. And I can't tell if they're struggling or if they just... don't care? I need advice. What do I do differently?
Tina
(immediately, warmly) Oh Andrew, three weeks is still early! I remember when I was training new techs in the field — it took at least a month before anyone could read a calibration log without holding it upside down. My advice? Positive reinforcement. Celebrate the small wins. Did Luke close a ticket today? That's a win! Did Anthony show up before nine-thirty? Growth!
Andrew
Tina, Anthony showed up at nine-fifteen today and he was still in his car.
Tina
(unwavering) But he was on time in the car! That's momentum!
Laugh track
Andrew
Devon, what do you think?
Devon
(camera off, voice like a man narrating a nature documentary about extinction) What do I think. I think you're asking the wrong question. It's not "what do I do differently." It's "how do I accept that nothing will change." I've seen twelve new hires come through this team in eight years. They all start the same way. Bright-eyed. Enthusiastic. Can't find the admin portal. Three months later they either figure it out or they leave. Your job isn't to make them learn. Your job is to survive until they decide to.
Silence.
Tina
(gently) Devon, that's the most depressing pep talk I've ever heard.
Devon
It's not a pep talk. It's a weather report.
Big laugh
Andrew
Jeff, you're being quiet. What do you got?
Jeff M.
(he has not been quiet — he's been typing furiously while walking on the treadmill) Okay so I've been listening and also simultaneously building a training efficiency tracker in Excel. Here's what I recommend. First: structured micro-goals. Don't say "learn the ticketing system." Say "close one P4 ticket by 11 AM with documentation that includes timestamps, steps taken, and customer confirmation." Measurable. Time-bound. Binary outcome.
Andrew
That's... actually not bad.
Jeff M.
Second: shadow sessions. Don't tell them how to do a ticket — let them watch you do one in real time. They need to see the workflow, not hear about it. Third: daily check-ins. Five minutes. Start of day. "What are you working on, what do you need." That's it. Fourth: I built a twenty-seven-page training curriculum with branching scenarios and a skills matrix. I'll send it to you. I already sent it to Brenda.
Big laugh

Jeff M.

Jeff M.
She will. I'm playing the long game.
Laugh track

SCENE 5 Act Three — "The Real Talk"
Afternoon. ANDREW has pulled LUKE and ANTHONY back onto a call. No celebration this time. He's direct but not unkind. He's figured out — the hard way — that the advice he got was all good advice applied at the wrong time. You can't give autonomy to people who haven't learned the basics. You can't celebrate wins that aren't wins yet. And you definitely can't hand a twenty-seven-page curriculum to two guys who can't remember to clock in.
