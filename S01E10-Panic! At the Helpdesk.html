SCENE 4 Act Two — "Tina's Hunch"
2:30 PM. The team is running on fumes. TINA has been on calls all day too — but between calls, she's been doing something no one else has had time to do: reading the actual patch notes. Line by line. Her field service brain — the one trained to read calibration logs and equipment manuals while standing in front of a broken machine — is doing what it does best: looking for the thing everyone else missed.
Tina
(DM to Corine) Hey — can you pull up the running error log? I want to see something. Every ticket I've worked today has the same error chain: auth timeout, then sync failure, then cascade. But the auth timeout doesn't match the patch notes. The 4.3.1 update didn't touch the auth module. So why is auth timing out?
Corine
(DM back, intrigued) You're right. The patch notes say 4.3.1 was a UI update and a database optimization. Auth isn't listed.
Tina
(DM) Exactly. But what IS listed is a change to the session timeout value. They shortened it from thirty minutes to ten minutes. "For security." Corine — what happens if a site has a legacy integration that takes more than ten minutes to complete its auth handshake?
Corine
(DM, slowly connecting it) ...It times out mid-handshake. Which breaks the sync. Which cascades.
Tina
(DM) WHICH CASCADES. It's not the patch itself — it's the timeout change! It's choking every site with a legacy integration! The fix isn't a rollback — it's extending the timeout value back to thirty minutes!
TINA picks up her phone and calls JUWAN on the engineering bridge.
Tina
(on the bridge, energized, the most alive anyone has ever heard her) Juwan — it's the session timeout. They changed it from thirty to ten in 4.3.1. Every legacy site is timing out mid-auth. Tell engineering to push a config override — thirty-minute timeout — to all affected sites. That's the fix. Not a rollback. A timeout extension.
Juwan
(checking, then) ...Tina. You're right. That's it. I'm telling engineering now.
Within forty-five minutes, the config override is pushed. The ticket counter starts dropping. 38. 35. 30. 24. 18. The phones slow down. The queue begins to breathe. TINA did it.
Open P1 Tickets 8 FALLING — TINA WAS RIGHT
Applause — loud, earned, sustained
SCENE 5 Act Three — "Under Her Wing"
4:30 PM. The storm has passed. The queue is manageable. ANDREW is at his desk, completely drained. He's been on calls for seven straight hours. His coffee is cold. His headset has left a mark on his skull that may be permanent. TINA pings him.
Tina
(DM) Hey — you okay? You've been going nonstop.
Andrew
(DM back) I'm fried. Honestly. I don't think I was much help to Luke and Anthony today. I was too buried in my own calls to actually mentor them through any of it. I just kept telling them "next call, next call" and hoping for the best.
Tina
(DM) Andrew, you kept the whole triage running. You held it together. But listen — I've got an idea. Let me take Luke and Anthony for the rest of the week. Not permanently. Just a couple of days. I want to show them something different.
Andrew
(DM) Different how?
Tina
(DM) You've been teaching them process — and they NEED that. But today showed me something. When everything was on fire, I didn't fix it by following the ticket playbook. I fixed it by reading the patch notes sideways and thinking "what doesn't belong here?" That's field thinking. That's out-of-the-box. I want to teach them how to look at a problem and ask "what is everyone else NOT looking at?" I think they're ready for that.
ANDREW reads the message. For a second, there's a flicker of something — protectiveness, ego, the instinct of a mentor not wanting to hand off his students. But he remembers Episode 9. He remembers Rachelle's words. He remembers Dolly learning to use the perch.
Andrew
(DM) ...Do it. They'll learn a lot from you.
Tina
(DM) 💛
The next day. TINA has LUKE and ANTHONY on a call. Her style is completely different from Andrew's — warmer, more exploratory, less "here's the process" and more "let's figure this out together." She's teaching them to think, not just to follow.
Tina
Okay, so — when I was in the field and something broke, the first thing I'd do is ask: "What changed?" Not "what's the error" — that's the symptom. "What changed?" is the cause. Yesterday, everyone was chasing the error codes. But the error codes were just the cascade. The real question was: what did the update actually change? And nobody had time to ask that because everyone was on calls. But someone has to step back and ask the dumb question. That's what I did yesterday. I asked the dumb question. And it turned out it wasn't dumb at all.
Luke
(actually taking notes) "What changed." Not "what's the error."
Tina
(beaming) Yes! Exactly! Andrew teaches you how to run the play. I'm teaching you how to call an audible when the play breaks down. You need both. But today — we're doing audibles.
Anthony
(grinning) This is like having two coaches with totally different playbooks.
Tina
That's exactly what it is. And the best players learn from both.
